Planets can be described as "rough" in various ways, depending on the characteristics of their surfaces. There are a few different ways to categorize planets based on surface conditions:

1.Terrestrial Planets:

Terrestrial planets are rocky and have solid surfaces. They can have rough terrain, which includes features like mountains, canyons, and craters. Planets like Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars fall into this category.

2.Gas Giants:

Gas giants like Jupiter and Saturn do not have solid surfaces like terrestrial planets. Instead, they are composed mostly of gas and lack the rocky terrain that could be described as "rough."

3.Ice Giants:

Ice giants like Uranus and Neptune are composed primarily of water, ammonia, and methane ices, and they don't have rough rocky terrain either.

4.Extreme Environments:

Some exoplanets (planets outside our solar system) have extreme surface conditions that could be considered "rough." These conditions may include scorching hot temperatures, extreme pressure, or intense radiation. So, when talking about "rough" planets, it's often in the context of terrestrial planets with varied surface features. Each of these terrestrial planets has its own unique surface characteristics, which can be rugged or rough in their own way.



Name
The two first discovery papers use the names isolated planetary-mass objects (iPMO)[9] and Free-floating planets (FFP).[10] Most astronomical papers use either of these terms.[11][12][13] The term rogue planet is more often used for microlensing studies, which also often uses the term FFP.[14][15] A press-release intended for the public might use an alternative name. The discovery of at least 70 FFPs in 2021 for example used the terms rogue planet,[16] starless planet,[17] wandering planet[18] and free-floating planets[19] in different press-releases.
